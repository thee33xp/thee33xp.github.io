Blog Entry:

War Is A Racket

Smedley Darlington Butler, a U.S. Marine Corps major general and two-time Medal of Honor recipient, is remembered not only for his extraordinary military service but also for his later years as an outspoken critic of war and imperialism. At the time of his death in 1940, Butler was the most decorated Marine in U.S. history, having served in numerous interventions across the Caribbean, Latin America, and Asia during an era of aggressive American expansion. His extensive service gave him firsthand exposure to the often hidden motivations behind military action, which he would later condemn in his famous speech and essay titled War is a Racket.

“I was a racketeer for capitalism...”

A key turning point in Butler’s activism came in the 1930s, with what has come to be known as the “Business Plot.” Butler testified before Congress that he had been approached by a group of wealthy businessmen and financiers who wanted him to lead a coup to overthrow President Franklin D. Roosevelt and install a fascist-style regime. Butler’s outspoken refusal and testimony brought the plot into public awareness, exposing an alarming threat from within and confirming for him the dangers of unchecked corporate power.

Butler’s experiences, combined with his disgust at the plot and his growing awareness of the corrupting influence of private profit in military affairs, led him to publish War is a Racket. In it, he argued that war primarily served the interests of a wealthy few at the expense of the masses, describing it as a “racket” that enriches corporations while soldiers pay the ultimate price. His condemnation of what he saw as “the only one in which the profits are reckoned in dollars and the losses in lives” resonated deeply and continues to be a powerful critique of militarism and corporate influence.

Through his personal transformation from decorated hero to critic, Butler highlighted the ethical complexities of military service and shed light on the underpinnings of American imperialism. His life and words remain a potent reminder of the importance of scrutinizing the true motives behind war and questioning the interests it ultimately serves.
