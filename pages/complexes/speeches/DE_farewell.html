January 17th, 1961

President Dwight D. Eisenhower gave his farewell address to the nation. During the speech he shared a few, solemn thoughts, with the audience, as he explained the standing America now had, on the world stage. This American might was not solely due to the material prosperity of the country, nor to the military strength; but to the dependence of prestige and leadership mindful of balance while using this strength within the international community. He spoke of the 4 major wars the world had found itself in over the course of the last half century, and the responsibility we, as a nation, had to continue the pursuit of progressing world peace and bettering mankind. That the basic purpose of our experiment in free government has been, and should continue to be, the fostering of progress in human achievement and the enhancement of Liberty, Dignity, and Integrity amongst peoples and nations. He also stated that anything less than this continued pursuit, would be unworthy of a free and religious people, ultimately leading to grievous hurt felt by the people home and abroad.

Furthering his explanation of balance in pursuit of progress, Eisenhower cautions us with a dire warning of future crises engulfed by the ideology of those who would wish to remove these freedoms. The pursuit of these noble goals is, and will be, consistently threatened, as he describes us, facing “... a hostile ideology, global in scope, atheistic in character, ruthless in purpose, and insidious in method.” In meeting these threats, Eisenhower advises us to avoid the temptation of reactionary measures without counsel of a broader scope first. He signifies the dangers inherent in irrational responses associated with singular solution thinking and points towards consideration in the need of balance within the economy, current and future prospects of the nation, and the government's duty to the people and the duties it imposes on them. “Good judgment seeks balance in progress. Lack of it, eventually finds imbalance and frustration.”

Of the general looming threats Eisenhower warns of, he mentions two specifically by name. The military industrial complex being the first, being the cause of costly overspending regarding the national defense budget. He pointed to the fact that more money was spent annually, on defense and security, than the net income of all U.S. corporations combined. He spoke on how massive the arms industry had become since the start of the century, as it was unprecedented and extremely influential, he recognized the imperative need for this development while also sharing a warning to the public hoping they would comprehend its potentially grave implications. The second specific warning Eisenhower gave in his farewell address, is time and an avoidance of the impulse to live only for the moment rather than planting seeds for future generations. He stated that by ignoring the long term impacts of policy, the precious material resources of tomorrow would be plundered and the heritage of their grandchildren would be lost. He hoped “democracy would survive for all generations to come and not become an insolvent Phantom of tomorrow.” He was worried public policy could become captive of a scientific and technological elite class, and that it was the responsibility of statesmen to integrate these discoveries and technologies within the principles of our democratic system, consistent with the supreme goals of our free society.
